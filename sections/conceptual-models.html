Cumulative risk models

Evans et al. (2013)
  • More broadly, the ACE study is a cumulative risk model.
  • Creates risk score by tallying number of distinct forms of adversity.
  • No differentiation for adversity type, chronicity, or severity.
  • Does not fit my own intuition but still has good predicitve power for later-life outcomes.
  • Mechanism usually explained by increased allostatic load.
  • Likely not a sufficient model for neurodevelopmental studies.

- More broadly, the ACE study is a cumulative risk model - This is the prevailing approach to conceptualizing adversity - In these models, all adversities are created equal and it is the number of distinct types of adversity that matters. - So a child who experienced physical abuse, sexual abuse, and witnessed domestic violence would have an ACE score of three. - And another child who experienced poverty, neglect, and maternal depression would also have an ACE score of three - My own editorializing here: - Saying that each distinct type of adversity will have an effect of equal magnitude on any developmental process in question doesn't accord with my own intuition - And yet, it's impressive that making that assumption still explains, in a predictive sense, these later-life outcomes. - Now if you ask why, you get a lot of language about disruptions in stress response system and something called allostatic load, which is a fancy way of saying wear and tear on the body through stress. - But the neurodevelopmental pathways through which adversity affects health haven't really been explored through cumulative risk models - I think because treating all adversity equally is not specific enough to consider neurodevelopmental mechanisms.

The dimensional model

  • ACEs reflect underlying dimensions of development environment.
  • Dimensions have different influences on neural development.
McLaughlin et al. (2014)     McLaughlin and Sheridan (2016)
- A more sophisticated risk model has been proposed by Katie McLaughlin and Margaret Sheridan. - In this model, it's not the isolated experiences that are important but rather the idea that ACEs reflect the underlying environment in which neural development is taking place. - This theory supposes that one can identify underlying dimensions of the environment and that different dimensions have different effects on development. - McLaughlin and Sheridan have proposed threat and deprivation as two initial dimensions - So experiences like physical and sexual abuse, witnessing domestic violence, and community exposure to violence all involve a threat of harm - And ACEs like neglect, institutional rearing, and parental absence are high on the deprivation axis. - Other dimensions are possible, these are just two, and I'll talk about that at the end. - And poverty or SES don't map neatly onto the dimensions. Within the dimensional model, poverty is a context that increases risk in all dimensions.

The dimensional model makes predictions!

- So it's not perfect, but hey we're getting somewhere. - We can now make predictions about neurodevelopment.

Threat

  • The brain has an adaptive response to being raised in a dangerous environment, influencing
    • threat detection / aversive learning,
    • salience processing,
    • emotional regulation,
  • which have established neural correlates, e.g.
    • fronto-amygdala, a.k.a. amygdala-mPFC, a.k.a. limbic system
    • insula-dACC
    • hippocampal circuits
- Threat experiences are predicted to influence neural systems involved in threat detection (aka aversive learning), salience processing, and emotional regulation. - And these cognitive processes have established neuroanatomical correlates - like the fronto-amygdala (amygdala-mPFC) system (a.k.a. limbic system) - hippocampal circuits - or the salience network

Deprivation

Huttenlocher (1979)
  • Pruning drives experience-dependent plasticisty.
  • Produces neural systems that are efficient for development environment.
- Deprivation in contrast, involves lack of social and cognitive stimulation - Expected to hinder development in much the same way as sensory deprivation. - So this picture depicts synaptic density across the human lifespan. - For the first few years of our lives, there's a proliferation of connections between neurons - And for the rest of our lives, these connections are pruned away - Our brains get more efficient based on the environment in which they develop and the experiences that they have had. We're getting rid of stuff we don't need in order to process information more effectively.

Deprivation

  • Pruning can be maladaptive if deprived of normal input
  • e.g. early visual deprivation in kittens, Wiesel and Hubel (1965)

- But this normal process of synaptic pruning can be maladaptive if we're deprived of input that our brains expect - One kind of heartwrenching example comes from early experiments on visual deprivation in animals - Early visual deprivation in kittens, (Hubel and Wiesel) - Blindfolds on kittens for early stages in development - Led to early and extreme pruning in visual cortex - The brain says, I'm not using this, I don't need it, I'm going to get rid of it. - I woudn't dare show you mistreated kittens in this presentation so let's all just take a moment to appreciate the glory of leg-stretch kitty and face-palm kitty.

Deprivation

  • Neglect and caregiver deprivation leads to a lack of experiences:
    • sensory,
    • motoric,
    • linguistic,
    • social,
  • leading to reductions in cortical thickness and functional changes in each of these circuits.
  • In particular, dimensional model predicts deprivation will alter structure in frontoparietal network that supports executive function.
  • In contrast to threat dimension.
- McLaughlin's insight was that social and cognitive deprivation would lead to the same kind of maladaptive pruning as sensory deprivation. - In this case, it might not be visual input that's lacking but social or linguistic experience, let's say. - And in particular the dimensional model predicts early pruning in the fronto-parietal network that supports executive function

The stress acceleration model

Callaghan and Tottenham (2016)
  • Caregivers buffer children's response to stress.
  • Provide "scaffolding" for emotional learning while fronto-amygdala circuits develop.
  • Absence of caregiving acclerates development of amygdala-mPFC.
  • Adaptive in short-term. Maladaptive in long-term.
  • Prediction: amygdala-mPFC connectivity in caregiver deprived children will look like those of adolescents or young adults in control group.
- Another modern adversity model is the stress acceleration model. - Posits that adversity accelerates the pace of neural development. - We express this idea colloquially when we say that someone is an "old soul" or that they "grew up too fast." - The presence of a supportive caregiver scaffolds emotional learning while the fronto-amygdala circuits develop. - Either the absence (in the case of deprivation) or insensitivity (in the case of threat) of a caregiver accelerates development of fronto-amygdala circuits. - Adaptive in the short term to compensate for species-expectant parental buffering of emotional reactivity. - Leads to suboptimal emotional functioning in the long term. - Specifically predicts that amygdala-mPFC connectivity matures more quickly - So caregiver deprived children should have amygdala-mPFC connectivity that looks like those of adolescents or young adults who haven't experienced that kind of adversity.
