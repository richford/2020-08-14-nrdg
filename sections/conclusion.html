Conclusions and open problems

- So I tried to go a little fast to leave time for discussion, but let me review and talk about open problems which will hopefully seed a discussion.

Conclusions

  • Not all adversity experiences are equal
  • Mixed-adversity samples are inconsistent.
  • Must distinguish adversity types and control for co-occurence.

    • Threat

    • Alterations to
      • fronto-amygdala circuit
      • hippocampus
    • Adaptive aversive learning

    Deprivation

    • Alterations to
      • frontoparietal network
      • frontostriatal function
    • Early excessive pruning
  • Evidence for global accelerated development, but not confined to amygdala-mPFC.
- So to recap, we know that different types of adversity shouldn't be treated equally from a neurodevelopmental point of view. - And that mixed-adversity samples yield inconsistent results. - So it's tempting to just see if we can predict ACE score using the tractome. And we might try that since it's simple. - But really, we need to distinguish between adversity types and control for co-occurence of exposures. - The dimensional model predicts that threat exposure will lead to alterations to the fronto-amygdala network and hippocampal circuits due to enhanced aversive learning. - And it predicts that deprivation will lead to early excessive pruning in the frontoparietal and frontostriatal networks. - And I'd say those predictions are mostly supported. - The stress development theory specifically predicted premature development in the fronto-amygdala network - And that isn't really supported, at least by the evidence in the review I've cited here. - But there is evidence for more global accelerated development. - There's longstanding evidence that children exposed to trauma experience puberty earlier. - And there are hints that the neurodevelopment is consistent with this. - As we develop, cortical thickness decreases and structural connectivity increases. - And that's exactly what we saw with trauma exposed children as well. - The Gur et al. study found increased FA in the uncinate, cingulum, and SLF. - They also build a machine learning model to estimate adulthood and found that the trauma exposed kids were more likely to be misclassified as adults.

Really Open Problems

  • Investigate dimensional substructure:
    • Are threat and deprivation really unitary?
    • Can we do a data-driven decomposition of adversity types à la Drysdale et al.
  • Expand dimensional model to include
    • loss
    • unpredictability
  • Effect of exposure timing
- So those are open problems that sort of fit within the existing framework of adversity research - There are other more exploratory questions that could be really exciting - We could investigate the substructure of these adversity dimensions. McLaughlin's main insight was that adversity itself isn't unitary and she proposed threat and deprivation as the first two dimensions. - Well, is there really any reason to expect that threat and deprivation should themselves be unitary. - So just as a cartoon kind of illustration, imaging that we have some sort of tree-based decomposition of adversity. The dimensional model currently slices at the first branch cut, where there are two dimensions. Would it be informative to cut instead at the second level, where the dimensions are further decomposed into subtypes. - And do we need to decide a priori what that decomposition should be, or could we use a more data-driven approach in the style of this Nature article by Drysdale et al. - Drysdale studied depression using resting state fMRI in a sample of about 1100 people. They started with BOLD time series from 258 spherical ROIs, created these 258x258 functional connectivity matrices, defined a low-dimensional representation using CCA, canonical correlation analysis, then clustered subjects in this low-dimensional representation. - So using this data-driven approach, they defined four novel subtypes of depression that transcended the current diagnostic classification scheme and better predicted responsiveness to transcranial magnetic stimulation therapy. - I would really like to use an unsupervised method like this to see if we can identify useful adversity subtypes that might be distinict from the a priori diagnostic classifications. - Another avenue is to expanding conceptual models to include loss and unpredictability. - We think loss of an attachment figure, either due to parental death or forced separtion, should be important for neurodevelopment but we don't know exactly how. - ABCD captures data on loss through its questionnaires but it reamins to be seen whether we can isolate loss from exposure to other adversity types. - Likewise, unpredictability is thought to be important to developmental plasticity. Maternal unpredictability and environmental variability has lasting effects on neurodevelopment in rodent models. - So it seems important to extend these models to humans but I'm really confused as to how to even measure environmental predictability. - There are also open questions about the importance of exposure timing. There's a concept of so-called "sensitive periods," which are developmentally specific periods of heightened neural plasticity, when certain types of environmental inputs are more likely to produce lasting changes in behavior and the organization of neural circuits. - These periods have been identified for sensory domains (remember the kittens), for language development, for face processing. But a big open question is whether there are sensitive periods for adversity. This seems more relevant for deprivation, where expected environmental inputs are lacking. For threat, there's not really a developmental period where the brain expects to experience threat. But still, even for the threat dimension, perhaps trauma could alter the timing of other sensite periods. - So that's a really complicated question that requires large-cohort, longitudinal data like we have in ABCD. And if you're interested I would love thinking with any of you about how to formulate questions about exposure timing, because it confuses me. And again I'm trying to differentiate between things that confuse me and things that confuse the field.
